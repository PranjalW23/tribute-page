About Franz Kafka

A visionary writer who delved into the human psyche and existential crises

Franz Kafka (1883 – 1924) was a German-speaking Bohemian novelist and short story writer, widely regarded as one of the major figures of 20th-century literature. His work fuses elements of realism and the fantastic, often focusing on isolated protagonists facing bizarre or surrealistic predicaments, incomprehensible bureaucracies, and the absurd. Kafka’s best-known works include "The Metamorphosis," "The Trial," and "The Castle." His stories explore themes such as alienation, guilt, and existential dread.

Timeline of Franz Kafka's Life

1883

Born on July 3 in Prague, Bohemia (now Czech Republic) to a middle-class Jewish family.

1901

Graduates from the German Charles-Ferdinand University in Prague with a degree in law.

1902

Begins working in a law firm while pursuing writing in his spare time.

1907

Publishes his first short story, "Description of a Struggle," in a literary magazine.

1912

Gains recognition for his short story "The Judgment" and begins writing "The Metamorphosis," which he completes the following year.

1913

Publishes "The Metamorphosis" in a collection called Die Betrachtung.

1915

Works on "The Trial," a novel about an individual arrested by a mysterious authority. The first version is completed in 1914 but remains unpublished during his lifetime.

1919

Publishes "The Penal Colony" and "In the Penal Colony," showcasing his themes of alienation and existential dread.

1920

Suffers from serious health problems, including tuberculosis, leading to increasing isolation.

1922

Completes "The Castle," a novel that remains unfinished. This work further explores themes of bureaucracy and existential anxiety.

1924

Dies on June 3 in Kierling, Austria, at the age of 40 due to complications from tuberculosis.

1925

Posthumously published "The Trial," which gains significant acclaim and establishes Kafka's legacy in literature.

1931

The Castle is published posthumously, further solidifying Kafka's influence on modern literature.

1940s-1950s

Kafka's works, particularly "The Trial" and "The Metamorphosis," gain prominence and become key texts in existential and modernist literature studies.
